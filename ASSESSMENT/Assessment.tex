\section{Product Name}

\textbf{PowerStake}

\textbf{Student Name:} Xanthea Vazey

\textbf{Role:} Hipster

\textbf{Primary Responsibility:} AI subsystem design, user-focused automation features, and intelligent decision-support systems.

PowerStake is a smart household energy management system designed to reduce electricity costs through appliance-level monitoring, automated recommendations, 
fault alerts, and a mobile dashboard. The concept combines low-cost sensing hardware with practical analytics to help households reduce standby energy waste, 
improve appliance efficiency, and simplify energy management decisions.

\section{Problem Validation and Engineering Design}

\subsection{Customer Needs and High-Level Objective}

Customer research identified that many households want lower electricity bills but often lack the time, motivation, or technical knowledge to actively manage 
appliance energy use. Standby loads from televisions, chargers, gaming consoles, printers, and kitchen appliances can create unnecessary ongoing costs. Existing 
smart plugs commonly provide remote switching or raw power data, however they still rely on users interpreting information and manually taking action.

Customers are more likely to adopt a system that converts power data into clear recommendations, warns of unusual appliance behaviour, and helps reduce wasted 
energy without increasing user effort. This creates a need for an intelligent analytical subsystem that improves household decision-making through simple and 
useful outputs.

\textbf{HLO-004: AI decision engine}

\textit{The PowerStake AI subsystem must convert appliance-level power monitoring data into actionable fault alerts and energy-saving recommendations that improve 
household decision-making during prototype testing.}

\subsection{Subsystem Architecture}

The AI subsystem functions as the decision-making and analytics layer of the PowerStake platform. It receives appliance-level monitoring data from the 
sensing and control subsystems through the residential Wi-Fi communication pathway. Its role is to convert raw electrical measurements into useful outputs 
including fault alerts, energy-saving recommendations, and suggested automatic shutoff opportunities.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ai_subsystem_architecture}, the subsystem consists of five stages: data ingestion, feature storage, model training, real-time inference, 
and output delivery. Incoming measurements such as voltage, current, power draw, switching state, and timestamps are processed into useful indicators including standby 
duration, inactivity periods, usage trends, and abnormal load changes \cite{Systemdesignhandbook}.

For the prototype stage, the training layer may use rule-based logic or lightweight machine learning approaches. The inference layer applies the selected model in near 
real-time to classify appliance behaviour and generate user-facing recommendations \cite{tensorflowlite}.

Outputs are delivered to the mobile dashboard, allowing users to review recommendations, receive alerts, and manually override suggested actions. User feedback may 
also be stored to improve future model performance. This subsystem provides the core intelligence of PowerStake by linking appliance behaviour with practical household 
energy decisions \cite{Systemdesignhandbook}.

\subsection{Trade Studies}


A trade study was conducted to determine the most suitable analytical approach for the PowerStake AI subsystem. The subsystem is required to 
analyse power monitoring data to identify appliance malfunctions, detect power-saving opportunities, and recommend automatic shutoff actions.

 with limited data, modest computational resources, and a short development timeframe.

Three candidate approaches were evaluated: (1) rule-based thresholding, (2) classical machine learning, and (3) lightweight deep learning.

suitability for demonstrating the AI subsystem value proposition.

HLO-004—Automatic Power-off Schedules  
A user-defined schedule will be configured through the system to automatically turn off a selected outlet (e.g. daily at 11:00 pm). The connected appliance will remain active prior to the scheduled time. The PowerStake system must execute the scheduled command and disconnect power to the outlet at the specified time.

The system will be tested over multiple scheduling cycles to verify reliability and timing accuracy. The test is successful if the outlet is turned off within the acceptable time window of the scheduled event and operates consistently across repeated trials.




HLO-004—Automatic Power-off Schedules
Criterion 4.1: The system shall execute scheduled power-off events within ±30 seconds of the specified time.
Criterion 4.2: The system shall successfully complete scheduled power-off operations for ≥95% of scheduled events over a minimum of 10 test cycles.
Criterion 4.3: The system shall correctly maintain and execute user-defined schedules without failure or missed events during continuous operation.

The Automatic Power-off Scheduling subsystem forms part of the wider PowerStake platform by linking power monitoring, intelligent control, wireless communication,

The Wi-Fi module enables two-way communication between the device and the mobile dashboard. This allows users to manually override schedules, restore outlet power, 
and review system recommendations. In later development stages, cloud-based analysis could be introduced to improve shutoff recommendations using longer-term usage trends.

The mechanical relay module achieved the highest total score and was selected as the preferred solution. Relay boards are inexpensive, widely available, and provide galvanic isolation between the low-voltage control circuit and the switched AC load, making them appropriate for prototype testing and early subsystem validation \cite{songlerelay}.

Solid state relays offer silent operation and long switching life; however, they are more expensive and may require additional thermal management depending on the connected load \cite{omronssr}. While technically effective, they were considered less practical for the current prototype stage.

Modified smart plug hardware ranked lowest due to proprietary circuitry, difficult modification, and reduced transparency during testing. Although these devices contain integrated switching hardware, they are generally not designed for redevelopment or subsystem prototyping \cite{tplinkplug}.

Overall, the mechanical relay module provided the best balance of cost, safety, ease of implementation, and suitability for demonstrating the subsystem High-Level Objective within the current prototype scope.

load \cite{omronssr}. While technically strong, they were considered less practical for an initial university prototype.

Modified smart plug hardware was the least suitable option. Although it contains integrated switching hardware, internal circuitry is often proprietary and not intended for modification, 
increasing development time and reducing transparency during testing \cite{tplinkplug}.

Overall, the mechanical relay module offered the best balance of cost, safety, ease of integration, and functional performance for achieving the subsystem High-Level Objective within the 
current project scope.
