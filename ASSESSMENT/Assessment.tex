\section{Product Name}

The proposed product developed by Group 40 is titled \textbf{PowerStake}. PowerStake is a smart power management system designed to help households reduce electricity costs through appliance-level monitoring, automated outlet control, predictive insights, and a user-friendly mobile dashboard. The concept combines affordable hardware with intelligent software to reduce standby energy waste, improve convenience, and identify potential appliance faults before failure occurs.

\section{Target TRL}

The current readiness level of the Automatic Power-off Scheduling subsystem is estimated at TRL 2. At present, the concept has been defined and supported through 
preliminary research into existing technologies such as smart plugs, programmable timers, relay modules, and mobile-controlled outlet systems. However, no integrated 
prototype has yet been constructed or experimentally validated by the project team.

Given the remaining project schedule, the realistic target for this assessment is TRL 3 progressing toward early TRL 4. This would involve developing and 
demonstrating a proof-of-concept prototype using commercially available microcontrollers (e.g. ESP32), relay modules, and basic power sensing inputs. Initial 
validation may be performed on low-voltage test circuits or simulated appliance loads rather than direct 240 V mains systems, reducing safety risk and development 
complexity.

A successful prototype outcome would demonstrate the core subsystem functions of detecting inactive loads, applying simple shutoff logic, manually restoring power,
 and communicating with a mobile dashboard interface. Supporting work may also include enclosure concepts developed through CAD modelling and early physical packaging 
 studies for future iterations.

Achieving a full TRL 4 mains-ready prototype within the current timeframe is considered ambitious due to hardware procurement, electrical safety constraints, and 
software integration requirements. A more practical near-term pathway would be completion of a validated bench-scale prototype during this unit, followed by future 
progression to TRL 4-5 through controlled household testing across common appliances such as televisions, chargers, gaming consoles, and kitchen devices.

\section{Problem Validation and Engineering Design}
\subsection{Customer Needs}
Many households waste electricity through standby loads from televisions, chargers, gaming consoles, printers and kitchen appliances \cite{energygovstandby}. While individual standby loads are small, 
cumulative yearly costs are meaningful. Existing timer switches require manual programming and do not adapt to user behaviour. Most smart plugs offer remote switching but still 
depend on active user involvement. Customers require a solution that automatically reduces unnecessary consumption while maintaining convenience. The system must be simple to use, 
safe, and allow manual override when required.

\subsection{High-Level Objective (HLO-4)}

The subsystem shall automatically recommend or execute outlet shutoff schedules that reduce standby energy consumption by at least 40\% during controlled testing, while allowing manual user override within 2 seconds.

\subsection{Subsystem Architecture}
The Automatic Power-off Scheduling subsystem forms part of the wider PowerStake platform by linking power monitoring, intelligent control, wireless communication, 
and user interaction. Power sensors provide real-time outlet usage data which is processed by the onboard microcontroller. A practical prototype could utilise an 
ESP32 microcontroller due to its integrated Wi-Fi capability, sufficient processing performance, and low-cost implementation \cite{esp32productpage}. As well as ACS712 current sensing modules, 
and isolated relay switching hardware to provide low-cost monitoring and automated outlet control \cite{acs712}.

The controller stores recent usage history and applies scheduling logic to identify repeated inactivity periods or standby behaviour. When suitable conditions are 
met, relay drivers disconnect power to selected outlets in order to reduce unnecessary energy consumption.

The Wi-Fi module enables two-way communication between the device and the mobile dashboard. This allows users to manually override schedules, restore outlet power, 
and review system recommendations. In later development stages, cloud-based analysis could be introduced to improve shutoff recommendations using longer-term usage trends.

\subsection{Trade Studies}

A trade study was conducted to determine the most suitable switching method for the Automatic Power-off Scheduling subsystem. Reliable switching is essential, as the selected component must 
disconnect outlet power when standby conditions or scheduled shutoff periods are detected. For the current prototype stage, the preferred solution needed to be low-cost, safe, easy to integrate 
with an ESP32 microcontroller, and practical to implement within the available project timeframe.

Three switching approaches were evaluated: (1) electromechanical relay module, (2) solid state relay (SSR), and (3) modified commercial smart plug hardware. These options were compared based 
on switching capability, cost, integration complexity, prototype suitability, and overall practicality.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{|p{3.5cm}|p{3.8cm}|p{2cm}|p{3.2cm}|p{2cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Option} & \textbf{Switching Capability} & \textbf{Cost (AUD)} & \textbf{Integration Complexity} & \textbf{Suitability} \\ \hline
Mechanical Relay Module & 10A, 250VAC switching contacts \cite{songlerelay} & \$8--15 & Low -- direct GPIO control, common modules available & High \\ \hline
Solid State Relay (SSR) & Silent AC switching, high cycle life \cite{omronssr} & \$15--30 & Medium -- load matching and thermal management required & Medium \\ \hline
Modified Smart Plug Hardware & Existing mains switching in consumer enclosure \cite{tplinkplug} & \$20+ & High -- proprietary PCB, difficult modification & Low \\ \hline
\end{tabular}
\caption{Automatic Power-off Scheduling trade study}
\label{tab:trade}
\end{table}

The mechanical relay module was selected as the preferred solution due to its low cost, simple control interface, and suitability for rapid prototype development. Standard relay modules can 
be controlled directly using ESP32 digital outputs and provide galvanic isolation between the low-voltage control circuit and the switched load \cite{songlerelay}. This makes them practical for early-stage testing and subsystem validation.

Solid state relays provide silent operation and long switching life; however, they are more expensive and may require additional thermal management depending on the connected 
load \cite{omronssr}. While technically strong, they were considered less practical for an initial university prototype.

Modified smart plug hardware was the least suitable option. Although it contains integrated switching hardware, internal circuitry is often proprietary and not intended for modification, 
increasing development time and reducing transparency during testing \cite{tplinkplug}.

Overall, the mechanical relay module offered the best balance of cost, safety, ease of integration, and functional performance for achieving the subsystem High-Level Objective within the 
current project scope.

\subsection{Trade Studies}

A trade study was completed to determine the most suitable outlet switching technology for the Automatic Power-off Scheduling subsystem. This component is required 
to disconnect power to selected outlets when standby conditions or scheduled shutoff periods are detected. As the project is currently at concept stage and aims to
 reach TRL 3 progressing toward early TRL 4, practical factors such as low cost, simple integration, safe operation, and rapid prototyping were prioritised over 
 premium commercial performance.

Three switching options were considered: (1) electromechanical relay module, (2) solid state relay (SSR), and (3) modified commercial smart plug hardware. These 
options were compared using the criteria shown in Table~\ref{tab:weights}.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{|p{5cm}|c|}
\hline

Reliability & 15 \\ \hline
Prototype Delivery Speed & 10 \\ \hline
\end{tabular}
\caption{Trade study weighting criteria}
\label{tab:weights}
\end{table}

Each option was scored from 1 (poor) to 5 (excellent). Final weighted results are presented in Table~\ref{tab:trade}.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{|p{6cm}|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Option} & \textbf{Weighted Score} & \textbf{Rank} \\ \hline
Mechanical Relay Module & 4.45 & 1 \\ \hline
Solid State Relay & 3.85 & 2 \\ \hline
Modified Smart Plug Hardware & 2.95 & 3 \\ \hline
\end{tabular}
\caption{Weighted comparison of switching technologies}
\label{tab:trade}
\end{table}

The mechanical relay module achieved the highest score and will be selected for the prototype build. Relay modules are inexpensive, commonly available, and can be 
directly controlled using ESP32 digital outputs \cite{esp32datasheet}. They also provide electrical isolation between the low-voltage control circuit and higher-voltage switching side, 
which is beneficial during early prototype development.

Solid state relays offer quieter operation and longer switching life, however they are more expensive and may require additional thermal considerations. Modified 
smart plug hardware was ranked lowest due to limited accessibility, proprietary circuitry, and added integration difficulty.

Overall, the relay module was considered the most practical and realistic option for demonstrating subsystem functionality within the available unit timeframe.
